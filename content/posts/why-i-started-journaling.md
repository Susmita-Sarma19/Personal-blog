## I Used to Think Journaling Was Not for Me

Every time someone recommended journaling I nodded politely and thought: I already know what I think, why would I write it down? It seemed like a lot of effort for the kind of person who enjoys processing feelings. I did not think I was that person.

I was wrong about what journaling actually does.

## What Changed My Mind

A friend described her journaling practice in a way I had not heard before. She did not call it processing feelings. She called it clearing her browser tabs.

The idea was simple: her brain, like mine, runs too many things at once. Journaling was not about insight or therapy. It was about writing down whatever was running in the background so she could close those tabs and focus on the thing in front of her.

That framing made sense to me. I tried it for 30 days.

## What the First Week Was Like

Uncomfortable. I did not know what to write so I wrote about not knowing what to write. That filled about three minutes. Then I started writing about the week ahead. Then about a conversation I had been replaying. Then about a decision I had been avoiding.

By day five the page was full before I realized it.

## What I Actually Do Now

Ten minutes, first thing in the morning, before I open my phone or laptop. I write longhand in a plain notebook. No prompts, no structure, no rules about what counts.

Some days it is a to-do list dressed up as a journal entry. Some days it is genuinely useful — I have worked through problems I did not even know I had.

## The Thing Nobody Tells You

You do not journal to remember. I almost never re-read what I write. The value is in the writing, not the artifact. The act of putting thoughts into words changes the thoughts.

That is the part I did not understand until I tried it.
