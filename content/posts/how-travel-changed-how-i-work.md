## I Took My Laptop on Holiday

Not in a hustle-culture way. I was between projects and had the flexibility, so I spent two weeks in a place I had wanted to visit for years and worked mornings while exploring afternoons.

It sounded like a compromise. It turned out to be an experiment.

## What Working Differently Revealed

At home, my workday expands to fill whatever time is available. If I have eight hours I use eight hours — not always well, but consistently. On this trip I had three hours in the morning before the day's plans began.

Three hours was enough. Not for everything, but for the things that actually mattered. The constraint forced a clarity I had not been applying at home.

## The Things That Did Not Happen

I did not check Slack constantly. Not because I was disciplined — I simply did not have the mental bandwidth to context-switch between a beautiful city and a work chat. The work got done and the chat could wait.

Nothing burned down. This was the most instructive part.

## What I Brought Home

The main thing I brought back was a question I now ask at the start of each workday: if I only had three hours today, what would I actually do?

The answer to that question is usually shorter than my to-do list. Now I try to do those things first before I fill the rest of the day with the noise that feels like work but is not.

## Travel as a Reset

I do not think you need to travel to learn this. But removing yourself from your usual environment strips away the habits you have built up around work — the routines that feel necessary but are mostly just familiar.

Sometimes familiar is the thing standing in the way.
