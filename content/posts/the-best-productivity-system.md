## I Have Tried All of Them

GTD with its contexts and projects and weekly reviews. Time blocking with colour-coded calendars. Bullet journaling with migration and collections and future logs. Elaborate Notion dashboards that took longer to maintain than the work they were supposed to organize.

Each system worked for a few weeks. Then life got complicated and the system broke down and I felt guilty for abandoning it.

## The One That Stayed

A piece of paper. One side only. Three sections: what I need to do today, what I am waiting on from someone else, and what I want to think about this week.

That is it.

## Why It Works (For Me)

Every other system I tried had too much surface area. The bigger the system, the more places something could fall through, and the more energy went into maintaining the system rather than doing the work.

A single piece of paper cannot hold everything. That is a feature. If something does not fit on the page, either it does not need to happen today or something else needs to come off. The constraint is the point.

## The Part About Paper Specifically

I tried digital versions of this. Notes app, a simple text file, a bare-bones Notion page. None of them had the same effect.

Writing by hand is slower, which forces more consideration about what actually goes on the list. And a physical page is visible without opening anything — it sits there on the desk and does not compete with notifications.

## The Caveat

This works for my kind of work: writing, thinking, coordinating. It would not work if I needed to manage hundreds of tasks, track multiple projects with dependencies, or collaborate on complex timelines.

But most days I do not need to do any of those things. I need to do three or four hard things well. A piece of paper is enough for that.
